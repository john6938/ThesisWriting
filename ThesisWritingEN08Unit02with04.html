Paragraph development

Activity 1

Read this introduction to the structure of a paragraph.

A graduation thesis is a collection of sections, such as introduction and method. Some sections harness visuals, such as tables and figures. Each section has a heading and each section contains one or more paragraphs. Paragraphs consist of one or more sentences. In academic writing, longer paragraphs tend to begin with topic sentences, and very long paragraphs end with a concluding sentence.

A typical academic paragraph begins with a topic sentence. This is followed by one or more sentences that support the topic sentence. These supporting sentences can be considered as providing evidence for the topic sentence.

The supporting details may comprise both citations and elaboration. Citations are when you refer to published research. Three common types of elaboration are description, explanation and exemplification (giving examples).

Graduation Thesis

Activity 3

Read this section carefully so that you can answer the questions set by your professor.

Each area of computer science and engineering adopts different conventions and guidelines to follow regarding format, layout, organisation, language and other conventions. The structure of your graduation thesis will depend on the type of thesis.

There are four common types of graduation thesis.

  1. practical (develop and evaluate software),
  2. experimental (hypothesis testing),
  3. theoretical (mathematical proofs) and
  4. empirical examples (observation and surveys, e.g. usability studies).

The following example of the structure of a graduation thesis is given as a guide and not a prescriptive model.

Section Details
Front matter Title, author, contact detail, superviso
Abstract Abstract
Introduction Background
  Hypothesis or research question
  Preview
Method Data identification
  Data collection
  Statistical analysis
Results Most important results
  Second most important results
  Third most important results
  Summary of results
Discussion Comparison and contrast, Explanations, Generalizations 1
  Comparison and contrast, Explanations, Generalizations 2
Conclusion Summary of most important Explanations and generalizations
  Further work
References Bibliography of cited works
